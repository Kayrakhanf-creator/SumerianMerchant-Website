Payment has weight: designing the balance scale

Every vendor sim has a moment the whole game hangs on. Scanning the barcode. Counting the change. Sliding the card. Ours is older: a customer tips a sack, barley hisses into the pan, and you watch the beam.

No coins, on purpose

In our period, southern Mesopotamia ran its daily life on barley. Silver existed — weighed, never counted — but bread, beer, and onions moved against grain. So the game has no coin purse and no floating damage-number of profit. A sale ends when the beam sits level and you say it does.

That last part matters. The scale doesn’t auto-complete. You stop the pour. You judge “level.” A little generosity builds a regular; a little greed builds a rumor. The mechanic is the morality system, which means we don’t need a morality system.

The weight set is a progression system

You start with small stones: 1, 2, 5, 10. Early trades are onions and flatbread, so that’s fine. But the city grows, and one day someone buys a bronze axe, and you’re stacking fifty-piece weights like a fool while the queue watches.

Bigger weights unlock as your standing grows. The day you first drop a single great stone where twenty small ones used to go — that’s the level-up feeling, without a level. It also quietly solves late-game tedium: high-value trades stay physical but stop being slow.

The three-second test

Every system in the game has to pass what we call the clip test: would three silent seconds of this be satisfying to watch? Barley pouring, beam tipping, the last few grains, stillness. We think it passes. The demo in October will let you judge — level, or not.
