Why barley? An economy you can hold

Sumerian Merchant runs on two kinds of money, and the difference between them is the difference between a wage and a windfall.

Barley is your salary

Barley is the everyday unit. Bread, beer, fish, onions — the whole rhythm of shop life is measured in it. It flows constantly and in small amounts. At the end of a good day the pile in your strongbox is a little taller, and you can see the day’s work in it, because you weighed every handful of it yourself.

Silver is your bonus

Silver — weighed, never counted — is for the trades that don’t happen every day: the pawn a farmer never reclaimed, the strange piece a traveler sold you cheap, the artifact a collector wants badly. Barley keeps the shop open. Silver is how your life actually changes. Late in the game, the interesting decisions are almost all silver decisions.

The state sets the prices. That’s a feature.

Everyday goods trade at state-fixed prices — historically true, and mechanically liberating. Because you can’t out-price your problems, the game isn’t about undercutting; it’s about volume, access, and judgment. More influence brings more customers and finer goods. Your pricing freedom lives exactly where it should: in the one-of-a-kind pieces that cross your counter, where nobody knows the right price and everyone has an opinion.

A drought will still move the market. A caravan will still flood it. The frame is fixed; the world inside it isn’t. ���
