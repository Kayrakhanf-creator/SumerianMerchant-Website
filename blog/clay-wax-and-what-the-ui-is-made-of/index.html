Clay, wax, and what the UI is made of

We banned floating panels early. If information exists in the world of the game, it’s written on something someone made — and the material tells you what kind of information it is before you read a word.

Three materials, three meanings

Baked clay is official and permanent. State price lists, tax notices, judgments, your complaints. Clay doesn’t forget, which is exactly the point — when the Guardian stamps a penalty into a tablet, you feel the permanence in the material.

Wax over wood is personal and erasable. Your daily accounts, shopping orders, running tallies live on a wax-coated writing board. Smooth it over, start again. Mistakes here are cheap. That contrast — cheap wax, expensive clay — teaches players which actions are reversible without a single tooltip.

And sometimes, rarely, you’ll be handed something written on material that isn’t from around here. We’ll talk about that another time.

The sky is the clock

There’s no HUD clock. A sun arc at the top of the screen becomes a moon; shadows in the shop stretch as closing time approaches. When the light goes amber, you feel the Guardian coming before you think it. Most of our UI work is like this: taking a number out of a box and putting it back into the world.

Cuneiform, for the record, appears only as decoration and texture — everything you need to read is in your language. We love authenticity; we love you finishing the tutorial more.
